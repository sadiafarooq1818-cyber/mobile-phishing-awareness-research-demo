# Research Proposal Summary

## Title

**AI-Assisted Mobile Phishing Awareness and Detection: A Quantitative Evaluation of Microlearning Warnings for University Students**

## Background

Phishing remains one of the most common cybersecurity threats faced by ordinary internet users. Mobile users are especially vulnerable because they often open links quickly, use smaller screens, rely on messaging apps, and may not carefully inspect full URLs. University students frequently use email, student portals, banking apps, social media and scholarship/job application platforms, making them a relevant population for studying mobile phishing awareness.

Many anti-phishing tools focus on technical detection, but user decisions still matter. A warning may identify a suspicious link, but if the message is too technical, too long or not trusted by the user, it may fail to change behaviour. This research therefore focuses on the intersection of phishing detection, mobile warning design and cybersecurity awareness.

## Research problem

There is a practical need for mobile phishing warnings that are simple, understandable and educational. Existing warnings may alert users, but they often do not teach users why a link is suspicious. Microlearning warnings could help users learn from each interaction by highlighting risk cues such as unusual domains, urgent words, password-reset requests, fake branding or insecure links.

## Aim

The aim of this research is to evaluate whether AI-assisted mobile microlearning warning cards can improve phishing awareness and detection accuracy among university students.

## Objectives

1. To identify common phishing cues in mobile links and messages.
2. To design a set of simple mobile microlearning warning cards.
3. To prepare a basic phishing URL detection demo using interpretable URL features.
4. To conduct a quantitative pre-test/post-test study with university students.
5. To evaluate changes in phishing awareness, detection accuracy, confidence and perceived warning usefulness.

## Research questions

1. What phishing cues are most useful for explaining suspicious mobile links to users?
2. Do mobile microlearning warning cards improve students' ability to identify phishing links?
3. Does AI-assisted risk explanation improve user confidence and perceived usefulness of warnings?
4. Which warning-card features are most understandable to non-expert users?

## Proposed method

The study will use a quantitative experimental design. Participants will complete a pre-test measuring their ability to identify legitimate and phishing links. They will then view a set of mobile warning cards explaining common phishing cues. After this intervention, participants will complete a post-test with similar but different examples. The study will compare pre-test and post-test scores using descriptive statistics and appropriate statistical tests such as paired-sample t-tests or non-parametric alternatives.

A small technical demo will support the study by showing how URL features can be extracted and used to classify suspicious links. The technical component will be kept realistic and interpretable rather than overly complex.

## Expected contribution

The research is expected to contribute to human-centred cybersecurity by showing how mobile warning design and interpretable phishing cues can support user awareness. It can also provide a practical research framework for combining lightweight AI-assisted phishing detection with cyber-awareness education.

## Feasibility

This project is realistic for a Master by Research because it does not require expensive equipment or restricted data. It can be completed using literature review, public phishing datasets, mobile warning mockups, survey instruments, and standard quantitative analysis methods.
